Venture Capital as Information Intermediary: Screening, Shaping, and Platform Formation

with Chun Zhao (Stanford GSB)

We propose a unified view of venture capital as an information intermediary — an institution that resolves early-stage market failure by producing and acting on information that no arms-length investor can access at scale. This intermediation operates through a two-stage process: at the screening stage, capacity constraints force VCs to rely on hard founder credentials; at the monitoring stage, close portfolio engagement enables soft-information production that reshapes the venture's competitive architecture. This dual mechanism generates a sharp prediction: VC investment should systematically transform portfolio companies toward platform business models, since platforms face a cold-start coordination failure that only a network-rich intermediary can resolve. We test this prediction using a novel panel of startup website texts from the Internet Archive's Wayback Machine, merged with PitchBook deal data and Revelio workforce records. We document five findings. First, platform is the single strongest word discriminating VC-backed from non-VC-backed firms across all industries. Second, an event study reveals flat pre-trends followed by a persistent jump in platform language at first VC investment. Third, website cosine distances trace a V-shape around VC entry — the empirical footprint of front-loaded strategic steering. Fourth, post-investment product drift predicts founder replacement, consistent with soft information triggering the VC's Bayesian replacement rule. Fifth, the platformization effect scales with the investing VC's portfolio network size and cold-start supply. Together, these findings reframe the jockey–horse debate: VCs act as trainers who leverage relational capital to reshape the horse's competitive architecture.

Remeasuring Private Equity Ownership in the U.S. Economy

with Amir Sufi (Chicago Booth) and Young Soo Jang (Penn State)

We argue that the prevalence of private-equity-backed firms in the U.S. economy is substantially underestimated in both public discourse and the academic literature. We construct a novel crosswalk linking PitchBook deal-level records to the National Establishment Time Series (NETS) using embedding-based entity matching, phone-number linkages, and LLM-assisted verification. The resulting dataset matches over 160,000 PitchBook companies to nearly 300,000 NETS establishments, capturing roll-up platforms, post-M&A integrations, and holding-company subsidiaries that conventional name-matching methods miss. We propagate PE-backed status through PitchBook's investment network to uncover the broader footprint of private equity ownership at the establishment level.

Media Disagreement and External Corporate Governance

with Haoting Dong (Tsinghua University)

We study how heterogeneous media interpretation of corporate disclosures affects asset prices and managerial incentives. We build a model with a strategic manager who can inject ambiguity into public announcements, heterogeneous media intermediaries who produce divergent interpretations, and investors subject to short-sale constraints. The model yields several predictions. First, media disagreement generates a price premium as short-sale constraints prevent pessimists from fully expressing their views (a disagreement premium). Second, managers endogenously choose positive levels of disclosure obfuscation to exploit this premium. Third, regulatory interventions that constrain media sensationalism simultaneously reduce disagreement and discipline managerial disclosure quality, establishing media regulation as an external governance mechanism. Fourth, higher institutional ownership attenuates both the disagreement premium and the incentive to obfuscate. We derive testable implications for abnormal returns, long-run reversal, trading volume, bid-ask spreads, and disclosure readability, which we plan to test using Chinese A-share market data around regulatory shocks targeting financial media.

Inverting the Market: Minimum-Assumption Discovery in Behavioral Finance via Agent-Based Modeling

Behavioral finance has produced dozens of theories — prospect theory, overconfidence, herding, the disposition effect — each explaining specific anomalies under distinct assumptions. Yet no framework asks: given observed market phenomena, what is the minimal set of behavioral assumptions needed to jointly generate them? This project formalizes the inverse problem of behavioral finance, the dual of AI-for-mathematics programs that move from axioms to theorems. We design an AI-driven agent-based modeling platform in which a rule-generating agent proposes candidate behavioral axioms, a simulator runs the resulting market, and an evaluator scores the output against known stylized facts. Occam's razor serves as a regularizer, penalizing assumption complexity. The goal is to discover which classical behavioral assumptions are redundant, which are complementary, and what minimal assumption sets can reproduce the empirical fingerprint of real financial markets.

Software

Wayback4AI

An open-source Python toolkit for large-scale historical website data collection from the Internet Archive's Wayback Machine. Designed for researchers who need longitudinal web text panels, the tool interfaces with the CDX API for metadata retrieval and supports batch downloading of historical snapshots. The internal research version supports proxy-pool parallelization for high-throughput crawling.
